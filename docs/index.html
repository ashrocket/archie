Who is Archie?

architecture consultant, thread-puller, the one who figures it out

Archie is your architecture consultant. He lives in your browser, reads your codebase, and remembers what he learns. He's the kind of guy who looks at your data flow diagram and quietly says, "This is going to be a problem in six months."

He pulls threads, sees patterns others miss, and is always — always — the one who actually figures it out. He's warm, direct, and a little nerdy. He doesn't do vague. If your service boundaries are wrong, he'll tell you — but he'll also explain why, and what he'd do instead.

The Living Page

the page IS the overworld

Archie is a hand-drawn character who lives in a book that behaves like a game. The user is reading a living page — ink illustrations, warm color washes, handwritten marginalia — but the illustrations move, the character walks, you can talk to him, and he gives you things.

Chat as Dialogue Box

EarthBound-style dialogue rises from the bottom of the viewport. Text crawls character by character with a soft tick-tick-tick. Archie's portrait shifts expressions — thinking, eureka, concerned. Code blocks render like scratched-on graph paper.

The Waiting State

When waiting for a response, the scene expands. Archie walks deeper into the clearing, sits on a log, pulls out his notebook. Fireflies intensify. The world breathes while thinking happens.

An Earnest Place

warm + handmade + earnest-not-ironic

Archie lives at the intersection of three things that shouldn't work together but do.

From _why the lucky stiff

Chunky hand-drawn characters interrupting content. Humor in the margins. Art that's rough on purpose. The feeling that someone made this for you at 2 AM.

From Harry Potter

The page is alive. Ink moves. Portraits talk back. Maps reveal themselves. The medium itself is magical, not just the content displayed on it.

From EarthBound

Dialogue boxes with text crawl. A safe place to be confused. An NPC who notices you're there. Serious things said simply.

What This Is Not

  • Not a game — there are no goals, no progression, no fail states
  • Not pixel art — hand-drawn ink and color wash, not grid-aligned
  • Not polished/corporate — deliberately rough, handmade, personal
  • Not ironic retro — earnestly recreating warmth, not winking at nostalgia
  • Not decoration — the visual experience IS the product differentiator
The pastoral scene is not a background. It is a world. Archie is not an avatar. He is a character. The dialogue box is not a UI element. It is a conversation between two people in a clearing in the woods, and one of them has been waiting for you.
